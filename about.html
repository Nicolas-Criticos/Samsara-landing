The Project

Bringing 16,000 trees
back to life.

My name is Nicolas Criticos. I'm an electrical engineer turned farmer, stewarding VrischGewagt Boutique Olive Farm in the Swartberg Karoo — one of the most beautiful and remote valleys in South Africa.

Thirteen years ago, 16,000 olive trees were planted here with a bold vision for abundance. At peak, the farm was producing thousands of litres of cold-pressed extra virgin olive oil per year. Then came drought, infrastructure collapse, and years of neglect. Today, less than 1% of the trees bear fruit.

I'm bringing them back — not with shortcuts, but with restored irrigation, soil rehabilitation, structural pruning, and years of patient, methodical care. Every bottle of Samsara olive oil directly funds this work.

Where we are now

5,000 trees. Halfway there.

The full farm has 16,000 trees across 40 irrigation blocks. I'm currently focused on the first 10 blocks — approximately 5,000 trees. Blocks 1–3 were the pilot: irrigation has been fully restored and the trees are responding. Blocks 4–6 have irrigation back online but need soil and canopy work. Blocks 7–10 haven't started yet.

The work involves repairing decades-old drip irrigation systems, rebuilding soil biology with compost and biochar, heavy structural pruning to redirect growth, and targeted nutrition programs timed to the trees' seasonal cycle. Even with everything done right, stressed olive trees take 2–4 seasons to fully recover.

With one labourer, the current pace covers about 2,500 trees per year. With proper funding and a 20-person sprint team, the entire farm could be rehabilitated in 1–2 years — with break-even projected by year three.
